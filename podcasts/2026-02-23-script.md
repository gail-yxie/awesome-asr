# ASR & Speech Language Daily Podcast — 2026-02-23

Host: Hello and welcome to ASR & Speech Language Daily, your go-to source for the latest breakthroughs in speech technology. I’m your host, and today is February 23rd, 2026. We’ve got a packed show today looking at a fascinating intersection of high-end research architectures hitting the real world of edge computing. 

Guest: It’s great to be here. Today is really about the democratization of these massive models. We’re seeing a lot of specialized fine-tuning and, crucially, a big push toward getting these models to run natively on mobile hardware without losing the performance we’ve come to expect from cloud-based systems.

Host: Exactly. The headline today is definitely the CoreML adaptation of NVIDIA’s Parakeet-TDT architecture. For those who haven’t been following the Parakeet lineage, this is NVIDIA’s push into high-efficiency transducers. But seeing it pop up in CoreML is a signal that the industry is very serious about on-device privacy and latency.

Guest: It really is. And for our practitioners out there, Parakeet-TDT—which stands for Token-and-Duration Transducer—is a bit of a departure from the traditional RNN-T or even the vanilla Transformer-based ASR we’ve seen. Instead of just predicting the next token, it’s predicting the duration as well. This allows it to skip redundant frames, which makes it incredibly fast. 

Host: Right, and we’re seeing two specific versions today from the user "richtext" on Hugging Face: the `parakeet-tdt-0.6b-v2-coreml` and the `v3` version. At 600 million parameters, it’s in that "Goldilocks" zone—small enough to fit in the memory of a modern smartphone, but large enough to handle complex vocabulary. 

Guest: And the CoreML part is the kicker. If you’re a developer building for iOS or macOS, you’re not just looking for a model that "can" run; you’re looking for something optimized for the Apple Neural Engine. Seeing these TDT architectures ported over suggests that we’re moving away from "Whisper-everything" for real-time applications. Whisper is great, but its cross-attention mechanism can be heavy for a live transcription stream compared to a transducer.

Host: That’s a great point. While Whisper remains the community favorite for offline batch processing, the real-time, low-latency crown is still very much up for grabs. However, speaking of Whisper, the community isn't letting it go anywhere. We have a very interesting release today: `ipa-whisper-medium` by neurlang.

Guest: This one caught my eye immediately. Most ASR models output standard orthography—actual words in a language’s script. But `ipa-whisper-medium` outputs the International Phonetic Alphabet. 

Host: Why is that significant for someone who isn't a linguist?

Guest: Think about language learning apps, or even speech therapy. If you want to give a user feedback on exactly *how* they pronounced a word, a standard ASR model might just "fix" their mistake and output the correct word they intended to say. A phonetic model tells you what they *actually* said. It captures the nuances of accents and mispronunciations. For a practitioner building an educational tool, this is much more valuable than a standard transcript.

Host: It also helps with the "out-of-vocabulary" problem, right? If the model is transcribing phonetically, it doesn't get tripped up by a new slang word or a brand name it hasn't seen before. It just writes down the sounds.

Guest: Precisely. It’s a lower-level representation that gives you more flexibility downstream. And it’s built on the "medium" Whisper variant, so it has enough capacity to handle diverse acoustic environments.

Host: Moving from linguistics to the broader trend of "multimodal" small language models, we have `Voxtral-Mini-4B-H2E-FineTune`. This name is a mouthful, but it represents a massive shift. Guest, we’ve talked about the "Cascade" approach before—where you have an ASR model, then a text LLM, then a TTS model. Why is Voxtral different?

Guest: Voxtral is part of this new wave where speech is treated as a first-class citizen inside the language model itself. Instead of converting audio to text and then feeding it to the brain, these models often use audio tokens directly. The "4B" in the name tells us it’s a 4-billion parameter model. That’s the new sweet spot for what we call SLMs—Small Language Models. It’s small enough to run on a high-end laptop or a powerful edge device, but because it’s a unified model, it can understand tone, emotion, and context in a way that a text-only LLM never could.

Host: And this specific release by frankmorales2020 is a fine-tune. It’s interesting to see how these base models are being tailored. In this case, the "H2E" likely refers to a specific task or dataset optimization. It shows that we’re past the stage of just wanting a "general" model. People want a model that can handle specific conversational workflows.

Guest: It’s the "specialization" phase of AI. We saw this with text models a year ago, and now it's hitting speech. 

Host: Let's look at some of the regional work being done. The report shows a flurry of activity in low-resource and non-English languages. We have `vietnamese-whisper-asr`, `whisper-small-fleurs-id` for Indonesian, and even `mms-1b-all-bemgen-combined` for Bemba. 

Guest: The Bemba model is particularly cool. That’s using Meta’s MMS—Massively Multilingual Speech—architecture. MMS was trained on over 1,100 languages. What we're seeing today is the community taking that massive base and fine-tuning it for specific regional dialects and languages that have historically been ignored by big tech. For practitioners in Africa or Southeast Asia, this isn't just a research curiosity; it's the difference between a voice interface working or not working for millions of people.

Host: And it’s not just transcription. We’re seeing Text-to-Speech (TTS) updates too. There’s a new `uzbek-girl-voice` and a Bulgarian fine-tune for `mio-tts`. But the one that really stood out to me was `ACE-Step-1.5-Naija-Legacy-Rhythms-LoRA-v1`. 

Guest: Oh, I love the ACE-Step work. This is getting into the "prosody" and "rhythm" of speech. "Naija Legacy Rhythms" implies that it’s not just about speaking Nigerian English or Pidgin with the right words; it’s about the musicality of the speech. 

Host: Right, the "vibe." If you’re a practitioner building a voice assistant for a specific culture, you can’t have it sounding like a generic robot or a Midwestern news anchor. It has to have the right cadence. Using a LoRA—a Low-Rank Adaptation—to inject that cultural rhythm into a base model is a very efficient way to do it. It’s a small file, easy to swap in and out.

Guest: It also points to the future of "Identity" in speech models. We’re going to see more of these "personality" or "rhythm" packs that you can layer onto a voice model.

Host: We also see a new dataset being released: the `preacher-tts-dataset` by Bateesa. This is such a niche but fascinating example of what we're talking about. Preaching often involves a very specific oratorical style—long pauses, rhythmic crescendos, a certain vocal grit. 

Guest: That’s a goldmine for TTS researchers. If you can train a model to capture the rhetorical flair of a preacher, you’ve basically solved one of the hardest parts of TTS, which is "expressiveness." Most TTS models are quite flat. They’re "reading" the text. But a preacher is "performing" the text. 

Host: It really highlights how the bottleneck for great speech tech is shifting from "raw compute" to "high-quality, specialized data." 

Guest: Absolutely. If you have 10 hours of a high-quality, emotionally resonant speaker, you can do more today with a LoRA than you could do with 10,000 hours of generic data five years ago.

Host: Let’s pivot to trend analysis. When we look at all these releases together—the Parakeet CoreML port, the 4B parameter multimodal models, the IPA phonetic Whisper, and the cultural LoRAs—what is the big picture for a developer or a CTO looking at this today?

Guest: The big picture is "Local and Literal." "Local" because the hardware optimization is finally catching up to the research. You can now realistically plan to ship a high-quality ASR or TTS system that doesn't need an internet connection. This is huge for privacy, it’s huge for cost, and it’s huge for reliability. "Literal" because of that IPA model and the specialized LoRAs. We’re moving away from "just getting the words on the screen" and moving toward "capturing the exact sound and the exact feeling."

Host: It feels like the "standard" version of ASR is becoming a commodity. Everyone has a decent English transcriber. The value is now in the margins—the low-resource languages, the phonetic accuracy, and the edge-case optimizations.

Guest: And don't forget the integration aspect. Models like Voxtral are telling us that the "boundary" between hearing and thinking is disappearing. In the near future, we won't think of ASR as a separate block in our code. We'll just have a "listener" that is part of the agent. It understands the words as they come in, in real-time.

Host: That brings us to our forward-looking statement for today. We’re looking at a transition from "Voice-as-an-Input" to "Voice-as-an-Interface." We’re moving toward a world where the speed of Parakeet-TDT meets the intelligence of these 4B parameter models, enabling devices that don’t just transcribe what we say, but react to how we say it, in our own dialects, all without our data ever leaving the room. 

Guest: It’s a great time to be in the field. The tools are getting smaller, faster, and much more human.
