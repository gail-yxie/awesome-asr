Guest: It really does! We have zero new papers in the queue today, which is a bit of a rarity for us, but the model releases more than make up for it. We’re seeing a massive push toward what I’d call "practical localization." It’s one thing to have a model that speaks "Arabic," but it’s another thing entirely to have one that understands the specific nuances of Tunisian Arabic or Haryanvi. Today is all about bridging that gap between general foundation models and real-world, localized utility.

Host: And it’s not just about language variety; it’s about deployment. We’re seeing a ton of ONNX exports and optimized versions of models like GigaAM and Whisper. For a practitioner, this is actually more exciting than a theoretical paper because these are tools you can go out and use right now. Let’s start with the big one—the localization of the Whisper architecture. We saw a flurry of activity around the "Turbo" and "Large" variants today.

Guest: Right, specifically the release of `whisper-large-v3-turbo-ko` by tellang. Now, for those who haven’t been tracking the "Turbo" evolution, this is essentially a distilled version of Whisper Large-v3. It maintains most of the accuracy of the large model but runs significantly faster. Seeing a dedicated Korean fine-tune is a huge deal for developers in that region. Korean is a morphologically rich language, and standard multilingual models often struggle with specific honorifics or fast-paced conversational speech. This turbo-optimized version suggests that we’re moving away from the "one-size-fits-all" approach and into highly performant, language-specific distillations.

Host: It’s interesting you mention the language-specific focus, because I noticed an incredible amount of work on Tunisian Arabic today. Specifically, an author named itsarunendra released four different versions of a Tunisian-focused Whisper medium model, ranging from version 1 to 2.1. Why do you think we’re seeing such iterative releases for a specific dialect like this?

Guest: Tunisian Arabic, or "Tunsi," is a perfect example of why global models need local fine-tuning. It’s a dialect that blends Arabic with significant influences from French and Berber. If you use a standard Whisper model trained on Modern Standard Arabic, the Word Error Rate is going to be disappointing for a local user. By iterating from v1 to v2.1, it looks like the developer is likely incorporating more diverse datasets—perhaps switching from formal broadcast data to more "in-the-wild" social media or conversational audio. For a practitioner, this shows that "finishing" a model is rarely a one-and-done process. It’s about constant refinement against the specific way people actually speak.

Host: Speaking of how people actually speak, we also saw a release for Haryanvi—`ASR_haryanvi_small_finetuned`—and several South Asian variants like `wav2vec2-svarah`. Haryanvi is often considered a dialect of Hindi, but it has its own distinct phonetic patterns. This highlights a trend where "low-resource" isn't just about the language anymore, but about the specific dialectal variations that large-scale web-crawled datasets usually miss.

Guest: Exactly. And look at the datasets that dropped today to support this. We have the `Pidgin_ASR_Dataset_Combined` and a `mental-health-luganda` dataset. Luganda is a major language in Uganda, and seeing a dataset specifically focused on mental health contexts is fascinating. It tells us that ASR isn't just being used for generic transcription anymore; it’s being tailored for sensitive, high-stakes domains where the model has to be incredibly precise. If you're building a health-tech app for East Africa, you can't just rely on a model trained on European news broadcasts.

Host: Let’s pivot to the "how" of deployment. We saw a really interesting release: `gigaam-v3-e2e-rnnt-onnx`. Now, GigaAM has been making waves as a robust alternative to Whisper for certain tasks, particularly in the Russian language space and beyond. But the "RNN-T" and "ONNX" parts of that name are what should catch a developer's eye. Guest, can you break down why an RNN-T architecture in ONNX format is such a win for production?

Guest: This is pure gold for edge computing. Most Whisper-based models use an encoder-decoder architecture with attention, which is great for accuracy but can be tricky for streaming. RNN-T, or Recurrent Neural Network Transducer, is natively designed for streaming ASR. It processes audio chunks as they come in and emits text almost immediately. By exporting this to ONNX—the Open Neural Network Exchange format—it means you can run this model on almost anything: an Android phone, a low-power IoT device, or a specialized server without needing a full PyTorch environment. It’s the difference between "upload your file and wait" and "see the words appear as I speak."

Host: And we’re seeing that same "efficiency-first" mindset with the `Qwen3-ASR-0.6B-ONNX-CPU` release. A 600-million parameter model optimized for CPU usage. That’s tiny by modern standards, but for a dedicated ASR task, it’s often more than enough.

Guest: It’s the "small model" revolution. While the headlines are usually stolen by 400-billion parameter monsters, the ASR community is realizing that for 90% of tasks, a well-tuned 0.6B or 1B model is the sweet spot. We also saw `canary-180m-flash-onnx`. 180 million parameters! That is incredibly small. NVIDIA’s Canary models are known for their performance in multi-task speech—ASR and translation—and seeing a "Flash" version in ONNX tells me that sub-100ms latency is the new target for developers.

Host: I want to touch on the "Domain Specific" releases too. There’s a model called `whisper-small-courtroom-en`. That sounds very specific. Why would a "Small" Whisper model tuned for a courtroom be better than a "Large" model that has seen everything?

Guest: Legal jargon and courtroom acoustics. In a courtroom, you have very specific turn-taking behaviors, a lot of formal terminology, and often, less-than-ideal microphone placement. A general model might hallucinate a common word when a lawyer actually used a specific legal term. Fine-tuning on courtroom data teaches the model the "vocabulary of the law." It reduces the need for manual correction later, which, in a legal setting, is a massive cost saver.

Host: Moving away from ASR for a moment, the Text-to-Speech (TTS) side of the house was also very busy today. We have multiple versions of `s2-pro` and a whole host of `qwen3-tts` variants. What’s the vibe in the TTS world right now?

Guest: It’s all about voice design and customization. The `deAPI-ai/qwen3-tts-12hz-1-7b-voicedesign` model is a great example. They aren’t just releasing a model that turns text to speech; they are releasing models specifically for "voicedesign" and "customvoice." We’re moving into an era where "cloning" a voice or "designing" a synthetic persona is a standard feature rather than a research breakthrough. Also, the `OuteTTS-1.0-1B` release is notable. OuteTTS has been gaining traction for being an open-weight model that produces very natural prosody without the massive computational overhead of some older systems.

Host: I also noticed several "MLX" versions of models like `maya1-mlx-4bit`. For our listeners who are Apple Silicon users, this is a big deal, right?

Guest: Huge. MLX is Apple’s framework for high-performance machine learning on Mac chips. Seeing 4-bit and 8-bit quantized versions of speech models means you can run high-quality TTS or ASR locally on a MacBook Air with almost no battery drain. It democratizes the ability to develop and test these models. You don't need a $10,000 A100 GPU to be an ASR researcher anymore; you just need a laptop and the right quantization.

Host: We should also talk about the `Covo-Audio-Chat` release from Tencent. This is tagged as "audio-to-audio." Are we finally seeing the end of the "ASR-then-LLM-then-TTS" pipeline?

Guest: We’re certainly moving that way. The "cascade" system—where you transcribe speech, feed it to a text model, and then speak the output—is always going to have latency and "emotional leakage." You lose the tone, the sarcasm, the urgency of the original speech. Audio-to-audio models like Covo or the "S2" models we see today aim to process the audio signal directly. It’s more like a human brain; we don't translate speech into a text transcript in our heads before we understand it. This "end-to-end" audio modeling is the next big frontier. It’s harder to train, but the results are much more fluid and human-like.

Host: So, if we step back and look at the trend analysis for today, what’s the big picture? No papers, 38 models, two datasets. What does this tell a practitioner about the state of the industry in early 2026?

Guest: It tells me that the "Foundation Model" phase is maturing into the "Implementation" phase. A year or two ago, we were all amazed that Whisper could do so many languages. Now, the novelty has worn off, and the real work has begun. That work consists of: one, making it run on a CPU; two, making it understand the specific dialect of a person in a rural village or a high-pressure courtroom; and three, making it move from a text-based interface to a native audio-to-audio experience. The "Daily" part of our podcast title is really fitting today because these are the incremental, daily improvements that actually build the products we use.
